What You Discovered

Smart Initialization

2-opt tends to find a shorter route when it starts from a Greedy-constructed tour instead of a random one. It won't always win, but the Greedy heuristic builds a good-enough tour while leaving structural "defects" that local search can exploit, giving it a consistent edge over many trials. In quantum computing, "warm-starting" follows the same principle: give a quantum algorithm a classically pre-computed starting point so it can converge faster. Researchers are actively studying how to seed quantum algorithms like QAOA with classical solutions.

Parallelization

By running multiple random starts in parallel and keeping the best, you improved your odds of landing near a good solution, but at the cost of proportionally more total compute. Each random route landed in a completely different region of the solution landscape, and 2-opt pulled each toward a different local minimum. The tradeoff between exploration and compute cost is central to optimization. A classical computer must evaluate parallel paths individually. A quantum computer, by contrast, uses superposition to represent a vast landscape of possibilities within a single quantum state. Through quantum interference, it then amplifies the probability of good routes while canceling out bad ones, navigating the problem space in a fundamentally different way.

Why This Is Hard

With just 30 stops, there are more than 1030 possible routes, a number with 31 digits. No computer can try them all. Every strategy you used (manual untangling, smart starting points, parallel exploration) is a way to search smarter, not harder. Quantum optimization algorithms like QAOA apply the same philosophy: explore the solution landscape more efficiently by exploiting quantum effects like superposition and interference.

These are open research questions. No person, no classical computer, and no quantum computer has found the best way to solve these problems yet. This is a frontier with room for new ideas, and yours could be one of them.

The problem you just explored, finding the best order to visit a set of locations, is called the Traveling Salesperson Problem. The same kind of combinatorial explosion appears in many places. Can you think of situations in your own life or work where you face a similar challenge?
